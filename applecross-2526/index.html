The Season in Brief
Thirteen wins from seventeen regular season games. Two 300-plus scores. A semi-final appearance. Angus Castley skippered the side through a campaign that saw them lose just three times — twice to FMPCC Pirates and once to WSCC in what looked suspiciously like a mid-season wobble. They responded both times by winning the next game. That's a captain's team.
Angus Castley: Captain
Castley led from the front all season — not just in the field but with the bat. 287 runs at 31.89, two half-centuries, and a composed 82 in the Round 1 win to set the tone early. When he wasn't available, Jamie Pursell stepped up as deputy — steady, reliable, and the kind of vice-captain who makes leadership look boring in the best possible way. Castley also closed out Round 15 with 65 in a 10-wicket demolition. A proper skipper.
The Road to the Semi Final
After five straight wins to start the season, two tight losses in Rounds 9 and 10 were a blip, not a collapse. The team rattled off four more wins on the trot heading into the finals. The semi-final against Swan Athletic — who they'd beaten by 63 runs earlier in the season — was one of those days where the bats didn't fire. Monument top-scored with 37 and Seen took 3-23 with the ball, but 101 all out was never going to be enough. Swan ran it down by 64 runs. Semi-finalists. Season over.
About That Grand Final
Here's the thing though: the week of the Grand Final, everyone knew it was going to be a washout. The weather forecast was grim. The outfield would have been unplayable. Applecross, with their superior cricket IQ, simply elected to lose the semi-final in advance and save themselves the inconvenience of travelling for a no-result. Efficient. Professional. Deeply strategic. We are choosing to believe this.
The Elephant in the Room
Ryan Carmody played every game. All 17. He stood behind the stumps every single time. 30 dismissals. No fielding award. We will not be investigating this at this time, as no one is willing to explain it with a straight face.
By The Numbers
A 195-run win over CVPCC (the biggest margin of the season), 3 centuries, the only five-for in the competition from Dissanayake (5-30), a 10-wicket win in Round 15, and a 300-plus innings twice. Not a bad year's work for a team playing their sixth grade cricket in Perth.

Season Awards Night

Best & Fairest — Joint Winners
Darren Monument
& Jonno Seen
Monument: 480 runs · Avg 60 · SR 107 | Seen: 15 wkts · Econ 3.97 · 9 catches
A joint award that makes complete sense when you look at the numbers. Monument was the run-machine the top order needed — a century, two fifties, and a 107 strike rate that said he was never just playing for average. Seen meanwhile was the quietly indispensable one: 15 wickets at under 4 runs an over, 9 catches in the field, and enough batting to keep you guessing. Best and Fairest — and arguably the most deserving joint result of the season. Two blokes who showed up every week and just did the job.
🎯
Bowling Award
Sakun Dissanayake
23 wkts · Avg 20.91 · Best 5-30 · 1 five-for · 15 matches
Sakun Dissanayake was the most dangerous bowler in the club this season, full stop. Twenty-three wickets — five more than the next best — including the season's only five-wicket haul (5-30, which is genuinely excellent). He bowled 612 balls and took 10 maidens. The economy of 4.72 is solid for a strike bowler. He was also, technically, a batsman this season — 19 runs across 3 innings — but we'll let the bowling do the talking. A deserved winner.
🏏
Batting Award
Darren Monument
480 runs · Avg 60.00 · SR 107.14 · 110 not out · 1x 100 · 2x 50
Darren Monument scored 480 runs at 60. In 10 innings, with 2 not outs, he made a century, two half-centuries, hit 54 fours and 9 sixes, and struck the ball at 107 — meaning he was never just accumulating. He was the top scorer by 141 runs, the top average by a mile among regular batters, and won Best & Fairest in the same breath. The batting award was, frankly, a formality. Monument was in a different postcode to the rest of the competition this season.
⭐
Hall of Fame Inductee
Matt Campbell
14 matches · 339 runs · Avg 26.08 · 14 seasons of service
Matt Campbell earns his spot in the Applecross Hall of Fame — and if this season's 339 runs from 14 matches is his swansong, it's a fitting one. A team man through and through. The kind of cricketer who holds the dressing room together as much as the scorecard. Fourteen games this season, never less than committed. Hall of Fame? Absolutely. He's earned every bit of it.
😂
The Fielding Award (presented to nobody)
Definitely Not Ryan Carmody
17 catches · 7 stumpings · 6 run outs · 30 total dismissals
In a truly staggering act of selective blindness, Ryan Carmody — who played all 17 matches, took 17 catches (16 of them as wicketkeeper), effected 7 stumpings, and assisted 6 run outs — did not win the fielding award. That's 30 dismissals. Thirty. The next closest is 11 catches from DJ O'Hara. Carmody is literally in a different sport. We cannot stress enough: this man's glove work kept this club in games, and he got to watch someone else accept a trophy. We're not saying it's wrong, we're just saying the numbers are having a crisis of confidence about it.

🧤 Ryan Carmody

His 16 wicketkeeper catches, 7 stumpings, 6 assisted run outs, and 1 outfield catch add up to more than the next three fielders combined. He played every game. He wore the gloves every game. He smiled through every wide and every dropped catch in the outfield. And then he watched someone else lift a trophy. The Applecross Fielding Award selection committee has some explaining to do.
