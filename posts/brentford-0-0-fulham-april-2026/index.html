§ 01 · The Story

Dominant first half. Toothless second.

There was a period, roughly between the sixth and the fortieth minute, where Fulham were the better side. You could see it in the structure, in the pressing triggers, in the way Brentford's midfield was being bypassed. Marco Silva's men moved the ball with purpose, built from the back cleanly, and created overloads in the half-spaces. It was, by the cottage's standards, encouraging.

Then Alex Iwobi limped off on forty-two minutes clutching his hamstring, and something shifted. Not immediately — the first ten minutes of the second half were tidy enough — but gradually the shape deflated. Brentford grew into it. Their forwards started to find pockets. Bryan Mbeumo was at his slippery best, rotating between the lines, and Nathan Collins had to work overtime at the back.

The final half-hour felt like siege warfare in the wrong direction. Fulham dropped into a low block and tried to hang on. Largely, they managed it. But only because of one moment — one brilliant, instinctive Leno moment — in the ninetieth minute.

"I think we had a good first half, we dominated. I think we got control of the game, the build-up was good. We had some good combinations."

— Marco Silva, post-match

Silva is not wrong about the first half. But football is ninety minutes, and the second half told a different story entirely. "Second half, we started well, but we dropped a bit. They pushed in the last 30 minutes. From the bench, we tried to finish the game but our build-up was not at the same level, and we suffered more." That's the honest version — and to his credit, he gave it.

A point away from home is never a disaster. Eleventh in the table with seven games to play, Fulham are safe, comfortable even. But the fans walking back to the Kew Bridge Tube weren't exactly singing. One goal in five games has a way of muting celebrations.

§ 04 · The Drought

One goal in five. When does it become a crisis?

It isn't a crisis. Let's be clear about that. Fulham are safe, comfortable, and collecting points. But the goal drought is real and it's worth naming. One league goal in the last five matches is not the form of a side pushing for European football.

§ 05 · The Keeper

Bernd Leno, ninety minutes of therapy

There are keepers who make saves. And then there are keepers who make saves that change the entire complexion of a match — that transform a 0–1 defeat into a 0–0 draw with one outstretched palm. Leno is, increasingly, the latter.

The Save — 90'

Ouattara received the ball in the left channel on the counter, drove at Collins, and got a clean contact across his body toward the far post. Leno, already committed slightly to the near side, adjusted and clawed it out with one hand. The expected goals model had that chance at around 0.35 — a one-in-three. Leno made it zero.

"These moments in the last minute are always the best. It was a good moment and it was important for us."

— Bernd Leno, post-match

Silva deflected questions about the near-miss with a smile. "I prefer to go in a different direction and remind you about the chances we had." His media management is impeccable. But everyone in that away end knew how close it was.

Leno has now kept ten clean sheets in the Premier League this season. His contract situation beyond 2027 is something the board should be thinking about very seriously.

§ 06 · Iwobi Watch

The injury nobody wanted to see

Alex Iwobi has been the engine of Fulham's midfield this season — the link between defence and attack, the player who makes things happen when the structure threatens to become sterile. Watching him walk off at forty-two minutes was the worst moment of the afternoon.

"First reaction I got from Alex — it's not so bad, but in the next 24–48 hours, we will be more clear about it."

— Marco Silva, post-match

Samuel Chukwueze came on and worked hard — his pace caused Brentford problems in the early stages of the second half — but his link play and defensive responsibility aren't at the same level as Iwobi's. The difference in how Fulham controlled the middle third before and after the forty-second minute was not a coincidence.

With seven games to go and a summer window on the horizon, the Iwobi situation needs monitoring closely. He is the player the side most cannot afford to lose.

§ 07 · The Bigger Picture

45 points. 11th place. Safe and settled.

Fulham go into the final seven games with 45 points and nothing to fear from the bottom three. That is worth celebrating, even if the atmosphere around the Gtech wasn't exactly euphoric.
